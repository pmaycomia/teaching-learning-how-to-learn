Focused Mode

  • In focused mode, our brain is concentrating on a familiar concept or idea.
  • We use it to learn, practice, or strengthen a brain pattern.
  • It is necessary for deep work, and helps greatly in forming a new chunk.

Diffuse Mode

  • In diffuse mode, our brain is in relaxed state. We see the big picture and it is the mode where we are highly creative.
  • The brain can access broad ranging ideas to understand a new concept using an existing one stored in our long term memory .

in summary...

We use FOCUSED mode when we have to study for exams, and DIFFUSE mode when we have to understand a new concept.

In order to be an effective learner, we have to use both modes alternately.

"So what is the solution?"

Pomodoro Technique

A time management method developed by Francesco Cirillo using a kitchen-timer.

How to use it:

  1. Choose a task
  2. Remove all distractions
  3. Set a timer of 25mins(default)
  4. Reward yourself with a break for 5-10mins
  5. Repeat

Working Memory

Imagine having four hands with four items in each. You have the freedom to use all these items at the same time. Handy, but if you need to bring all these items to another place, you'd have to walk around with your four hands full.

Now let's compare it to the long-term memory.

Long Term Memory

Well since you are a smart person, you figured out you can store all your essential items in a thing which can hold multiple items. A bag! Yes!

You can use your items whenever and wherever you need it. And they can stay in your bag as long as you need them to be.

in summary...

Working memory can store concepts/thoughts/ideas in you brain for a limited time. If you want to access them again some time later, you'd have to store them in your long-term memory.

"But how do you store them in your long-term memory?"

Spaced Repetition

An evidence-based learning technique that is usually performed with flashcards. Newly introduced and more difficult flashcards are shown more frequently, while older and less difficult flashcards are shown less frequently in order to exploit the psychological spacing effect.

The highly recommended mobile and desktop application for spaced repetition is Anki .

Oops!

Another thing you should know about your working memory is that there are limited slots in it, pressumably four!

What does that mean for you? It means that you can only use or access four slots in your brain at a time.

That doesn't sound a lot. But the good thing is, we can compress related information to make it compact and fit into only one slot. This process is called chunking .

Chunks

Compact packages of information that the brain can easily access.

"How can I form a chunk?

How to form a chunk?

  1. You need total focus so avoid distractions.
  2. Understand the basic concept/idea.
  3. Gain context by:
    • - Seeing the big picture
    • - Repeating & practicing
    • - Knowing WHEN to use, and WHEN NOT to use

What else can help with your learning journey

Recall

Instead of rereading what you're trying to learn, the best practice is to explain the idea to yourself to see if you really grasped the idea.

Mini-test

Challenge yourself regularly with tests to know how much you actually know with the subject and to avoid the illusions of learning.

...more

Interleaving

Try to recall or review ideas anywhere else besides your usual place of study to get your brain used to accessing the information whenever and wherever you need it.

Exercise

It is scientifically proven that your brain can also benefit from exercise, not just your body. So move that sexy-ass of yours!

Sleep

This is the time when your brain removes the toxins and keeps the meaningful information produced in a day. Of course it is important to have not just 7 hours of minimum sleep, but also a quality sleep.
